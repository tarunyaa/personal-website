Building's Place in Maslow's Hierarchy

Can the difference in human agency — the capacity to build, create, choose — be explained by varying biological and social initial conditions?

Building sits near the top of Maslow's hierarchy. It is a form of self-actualization. To reach it, the lower rungs — safety, belonging, esteem — have to be reasonably steady. When a person's life lacks safety or emotional stability, their brain spends its energy on survival and regulation rather than on long-term agency and growth. There is no surplus left to build with.

One of the hardest truths is that many highly agentic builders became that way in part because they were rewarded early. They had stable attachment, supportive environments, and experienced competence young. The foundation was laid before the building began.

Chronic stress can really impair an individual — psychologically regress them, pulling them back down the hierarchy and out of the level where building is even possible.

Agency itself looks different across people. For some it shows up as conventional markers of success — career, wealth. For others it's emotional resilience, caregiving, holding a family together. The experience of effort is uneven too: what feels manageable to one person can feel overwhelming to another. The same act of building does not cost everyone the same.

So when we admire a builder, we are partly admiring the conditions that made building possible for them. That doesn't take anything away from the person. But it should change how generously we read people who haven't built yet.
